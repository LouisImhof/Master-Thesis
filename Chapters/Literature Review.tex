\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Introduction to Empirical Asset Pricing and Market Efficiency}
The core objective of empirical asset pricing is to understand the behavior of risk premiums and forecast cross-sectional asset returns, an endeavor fundamentally constrained by the degree of market efficiency \parencite{Gu2020}. Neoclassical finance, anchored by the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), posits that asset prices rapidly incorporate all available information. In its weak form, the EMH asserts that historical price and volume data cannot be used to systematically generate abnormal returns, theoretically rendering technical analysis ineffective \parencite{Park2007, Jiang2023}.

However, the proliferation of empirical anomalies over the past several decades has challenged this paradigm. Seminal behavioral finance literature has demonstrated that investor psychology and cognitive biases lead to predictable deviations from fundamental value. Overreaction to news drives long-term price reversals \parencite{DeBondt1985}, while the short-term momentum effect shows that stocks with recent strong performance continue to outperform \parencite{Jegadeesh1993}. These anomalies thrive particularly in environments characterized by high information uncertainty and hard-to-value assets, suggesting that investor sentiment directly influences the cross-section of stock returns \parencite{Baker2006}. While these findings legitimized the study of price trends, traditional asset pricing has struggled to capture the full complexity of market dynamics using standard linear methods.

\subsection{The Factor Zoo and the Transition to Machine Learning}
The academic pursuit of return predictability resulted in the discovery of hundreds of cross-sectional predictors, leading to a crowded "factor zoo" \parencite{Gu2020}. Traditional econometric approaches, such as Fama-MacBeth regressions and standard portfolio sorts, face severe limitations when evaluating this high-dimensional feature space. Linear models are highly susceptible to overfitting, multicollinearity, and are generally incapable of capturing complex, interactive relationships between variables \parencite{Gu2020}. Furthermore, rigorous replication studies indicate that a vast majority of these predefined factors fail to survive out-of-sample testing or adjustments for microcaps and transaction costs, raising concerns about extensive data-snooping \parencite{Hou2020}.

To circumvent the limitations of traditional models, recent literature has pivoted toward statistical and machine learning (ML) frameworks. There is foundational evidence that non-linear machine learning models, specifically regression trees and neural networks, vastly outperform linear approaches in out-of-sample stock return prediction \parencite{Gu2020}. The superior performance of ML stems from its ability to automate variable selection, reduce dimensionality, and dynamically capture non-linear interactions without requiring researchers to pre-specify the functional form of the data \parencite{Gu2020}.

\subsection{Image-Based Deep Learning and Price Trend Recognition}
While numerical ML models represented a significant advancement, a new frontier in asset pricing applies Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) directly to visual representations of market data, bridging the gap between computer vision and financial forecasting. For decades, technical analysis relied on human cognition to identify geometric patterns (e.g., candlestick formations) in price charts \parencite{Park2007, Tsinaslanidis2020}. By converting historical Open, High, Low, Close, and Volume (OHLCV) time-series data into two-dimensional pixel arrays, deep learning models can automate and optimize this visual pattern recognition \parencite{Jiang2023, Bang2023}.

Representing time-series data as an image allows CNNs to capture complex spatial relationships between price curves that traditional 1D methods obscure \parencite{Jiang2023}. Crucially, the vertical dimension of an OHLC image naturally encodes volatility; the high-low range of a price bar is a proven, highly accurate estimator of realized daily volatility \parencite{Andersen1998}. Consequently, a 2D chart image simultaneously feeds the neural network data regarding directional trends, momentum, and volatility structures without the need for manual feature engineering.

This methodology was formalized by training a CNN to predict the probability of positive future returns based entirely on images of past price charts \parencite{Jiang2023}. Their findings represent a paradigm shift: image-based CNN forecasts yield highly profitable out-of-sample trading strategies that significantly outperform stalwarts of the literature, such as traditional momentum and short-term reversal factors. Furthermore, their study demonstrates context independence, showing that U.S.-trained CNN models maintain predictive power across different time horizons and international markets.

\subsection{Network Evolutions and Transfer Learning}
The literature on CNN-based forecasting is rapidly evolving to incorporate more sophisticated architectures and diverse feature sets. Recent studies have integrated Graph Convolutional Networks to map the correlations and spillover effects between individual stocks \parencite{Chen2021Graph}, and employed Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) layers alongside CNNs to better capture sequential time dependencies \parencite{Chen2021, Liu2020}. Furthermore, the integration of attention mechanisms enables neural networks to focus dynamically on the most predictive segments of a candlestick chart, improving directional accuracy \parencite{Zhang2019}.

Despite these architectural advancements and the foundational success of recent image-based studies \parencite{Jiang2023}, the literature remains heavily skewed toward U.S. and Asian equity markets. There is a distinct lack of large-sample, out-of-sample evaluations of image-based CNN models applied to major European indices, such as the Euro Stoxx 50. Additionally, while transfer learning is increasingly recognized as a vital tool in financial data predictions \parencite{Gu2020, Jiang2023}, its efficacy,specifically training a model on deep U.S. market data and deploying it to predict European equities or highly volatile alternative assets like cryptocurrency, remains under-researched.

\subsection{Conclusion and Hypothesis Development}
The trajectory of the literature clearly establishes that while traditional linear models and predefined anomalies fall short of capturing modern market dynamics, deep learning offers a highly robust alternative. By leveraging CNNs to analyze stock price images, researchers bypass the limitations of the "factor zoo" and capture complex, non-linear trend and volatility structures directly from raw market data.

This thesis builds directly upon this theoretical and methodological foundation. By replicating recent image-based frameworks \parencite{Jiang2023} on the Euro Stoxx 50, and testing the transferability of U.S.-trained patterns to both European equities and cryptocurrency markets, this research addresses a critical geographic and asset-class gap in the literature. It is hypothesized that image-based CNN models will outperform traditional baseline factors in European markets, and that models pre-trained on the extensive history of the U.S. market will successfully transfer to European equities, demonstrating the universality of machine-learned price trends.
